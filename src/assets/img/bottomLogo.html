Crunchyroll Leads By Example: The Future Of Streaming Video Is Niche

Lauren OrsiniSenior Contributor
Opinions expressed by Forbes Contributors are their own.
Games
I write about the business of fandom.
This article is more than 2 years old.
  • Share to Facebook
  • Share to Twitter
  • Share to Linkedin

Streaming video companies: perhaps you’re offering too much.

Just look at Crunchyroll, a subset of Ellation that provides nothing but East Asian media to a global audience—and has 750,000 paid subscribers for its trouble. More than 20 million registered users watch Japanese cartoons, like Naruto Shippuden and Attack on Titan , and Asian dramas, like Boys Over Flowers, in seven languages from English to Arabic. Together, those users watch 1.5 billion minutes of streaming video every month.

Tom Pickett, CEO of Ellation, credits this vertical approach to Crunchyroll’s success. Rather than attempt to provide everything to everyone, Crunchyroll dives deep into just one category: Asian TV and animated cartoons, and from there has expanded its following by providing niche extras like news, forums, e-commerce, live events, and manga, all on the same website.

The main characters of Naruto Shippuden, one of Crunchyroll's most popular shows.

“General audience services like Netflix and Hulu forever changed the way we consume media and have helped educate users that there are new services worth paying for,” said Pickett. “But we have found there are other ways to create value for passionate fans by going deep in a particular genre and building community. Nobody knows this space like we do, and so, we’re able to provide our fans with a premium viewing and community-driven experience.”

The Crunchyroll experience also extends beyond the site itself. Crunchyroll staff attend dozens of anime fan conventions every year and offer perks. At Anime Boston 2015, for example, Crunchyroll provided a “green room” exclusive lounge for subscribers while also holding contests, events and panels for all attendees. These events, like Crunchyroll, target one of advertising’s most coveted demographics: teens. 75% of Crunchyroll subscribers are under the age of 35, with the median free viewer aged at 18, according to the company.

Crunchyroll's media library features hundreds of animated shows from Japan.

Crunchyroll’s biggest competition are sites like Funimation and Daisuki, which also provide streaming anime, related merchandise, anime news and forums for fans. So in order to get a leg up on the competition, Crunchyroll has differentiated itself by partnering directly with anime studios to co-produce some of the shows it streams. It’s a method no other competitor has tried just yet and if it works, it will give Crunchyroll prime streaming rights over its competitors to some shows, plus a modicum of control over the quality of the video it can offer.

Crunchyroll co-founder and general manager Kun Gao notes that the company already has strong partnerships with Sony Playstation, Microsoft Xbox, Apple, and Loot Crate, and working with anime creators themselves is a clear next step. At the same time, Crunchyroll continues to expand its growing library, featuring tens of thousands of episodes fans can watch for free (if they don’t mind the commercials) or beginning at $6.95 a month.

“We continue to super-serve our audience by curating the best and most complete content experience,” said Gao. “We’ve done that through deep relationship with our Japanese partners and careful curation of the content.”

Ellation is already harnessing Crunchyroll’s fan-first success for another community under its umbrella. Creativebug provides craft enthusiasts with project tutorials and “embodies our larger mission of super-serving passionate fan communities,” Pickett said.

It’s just another example of how, for this media company, less is definitely more.
